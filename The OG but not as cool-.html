A Little History

History of the Light Bulb

Before Thomas Edison entered the scene, and began commercializing his light bulb, British inventors proved that electric light was possible with the 'arc lamp'. In 1835, the first constant electric light was shown, then, for the next 40 years, scientists around the world worked on the 'incandescent lamp', working with the filament(See How it Works).

When Edison and his team of researchers started their efforts on lighting, they focused on improving the filament. First, they tested carbon, then platinum, before eventually returning to carbon. By October 1879, Edisonâ€™s team had created a light bulb with a filament of uncoated cotton thread that lasted for 14.5 hours.

They then experimented with more filaments until desciding to work on one made from bamboo that lasted up to 1,200 hours. That filament became the standard for the 'Edison bulb' for the next 10 years.

How it Works

How Does a Light Bulb Work?

Light Bulbs convert electricla energy to An electric current is passed through a thin metal filament, heating the filament to a high temperature until it glows and produces light.

After the electricity has made its way through the tungsten filament, it goes down another wire and out of the bulb via the metal portion at the side of the socket.

"When the battery's terminals are connected through the light bulb, a pathway is created for charge to flow. Negatively charged electrons flow from the - terminal to the + terminal through the light bulb. This flow of charge converts chemical potential energy into electrical energy."(https://courses.engr.illinois.edu)
